Letters from the Long Dark

08 Apr 2026

Winter is a kind of honesty. It strips the landscape of pretense, leaves only what can survive the cold. I'm learning to do the same.

This is my fifth winter in the north. I came for reasons I no longer remember clearly — something about escape, something about starting over. The seasons have a way of erasing those tidy narratives. By December, the sun rises at eight and sets at four, and the hours in between are a long, grey dusk. You learn to live in that half-light or you leave.

I stayed.

There is a particular silence to a winter morning that exists nowhere else. It is not the absence of sound but the presence of cold — a quality of stillness that feels almost physical. The trees hold it in their branches. The roads hold it in their frost. Walking through it feels like walking through a held breath.

I write letters in the winter. I do not send them. They are addressed to people I used to know, places I used to live, versions of myself I have since outgrown. I write them by hand at a desk by the window, watching the light shift across the snow. The letters are not confessions or apologies. They are more like records of departure — notes on who I was and who I am becoming.

Spring always comes, of course. It is not the point. The point is that winter teaches you something no other season can: how to be still, how to wait, how to find warmth in small and ordinary things. A cup of coffee. A good sentence. A window that faces south.

I will leave this place eventually. But I will carry the long dark with me, like a letter I never finished writing, like a cold that got into my bones and decided to stay.
