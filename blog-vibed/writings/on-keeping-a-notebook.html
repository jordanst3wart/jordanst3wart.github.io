On Keeping a Notebook

03 May 2026

The leather creases along the spine like a map of small decisions. I fill pages the way others fill silence — not because I have something to say, but because the saying of it makes room.

There is a particular kind of courage in the blank page. It asks nothing of you and expects everything. Some days I sit with it for an hour before a single word arrives. Other days the words come so fast I can barely keep up, my handwriting deteriorating into something that looks like a seismograph reading.

I have been keeping notebooks for fifteen years. The early ones are embarrassing — full of performed angst and borrowed ideas. But I keep them anyway, because they remind me that you have to write badly before you can write honestly. The bad writing is the scaffolding. You take it down once the building stands.

What I have learned is this: a notebook is not a record of what happened. It is a record of what you noticed. And noticing is a discipline, a muscle you exercise or lose. The more I write, the more I see. The more I see, the more there is to write about.

The best notebooks are the ones that look like nothing from the outside. Plain covers, unremarkable bindings. A stranger would never guess what they contain — the small griefs, the unexpected beauties, the sentences that arrived fully formed and then disappeared before they could be caught. The notebook is a net for those disappearing things.

I fill one every three months now. They sit on a shelf in my study, spines labelled by season and year. I rarely open them. But I like knowing they are there. A record of having paid attention. A promise that I will keep doing so.
