Notes on Forgetting

10 Mar 2026

The way a door left ajar still holds the shape of the person who passed through it — that is what memory is to me. A trace. A temperature. A hinge that still knows how to swing.

I have been thinking about forgetting as a creative act. We think of memory as storage, a archive of everything that happened. But forgetting is not failure. It is editing. The mind cuts what the heart cannot use.

My grandmother forgot my name at the end. She called me by her brother's name, by the name of a dog she had as a girl, by names I had never heard before. At first I corrected her. Then I stopped. I realised she was not losing memories — she was making new ones, assembling a version of the world that made sense to her. In her last months, she lived in a place where I was everyone she had ever loved, all at once. There is a kind of generosity in that kind of forgetting.

I keep a list of things I want to forget. Not the traumatic things — those take care of themselves — but the small ones. The exact phrasing of an argument. The look on someone's face when I said the wrong thing. A mistake I made five years ago that still wakes me up at 3 a.m. I want to forget these things the way a river forgets the shape of the rock it has worn smooth. I want the forgetting to be a kind of forgiveness.

But I also keep a list of things I am afraid of forgetting. The sound of my father's laugh. The way light fell across a particular table in a particular kitchen on a particular morning. A sentence a friend once said to me that I did not understand until years later. These are the hinges I do not want to let go of.

Memory, I think, is not about accuracy. It is about meaning. We remember what matters, even if the details shift. We forget what does not, even if it happened exactly as recorded. The door stays open because something is still passing through it. And when the passage is complete, the door closes on its own.
